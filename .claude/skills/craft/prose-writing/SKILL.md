# Prose Writing (blog craft reference)

> Adapted 2026-05-28 from `haowjy/creative-writing-skills` (`skills/prose-writing`),
> reframed from fiction (psychic distance, free indirect discourse, POV) to opinionated
> best-ball strategy writing. Reference module read during drafting — not a user-invocable skill.

These are the line-level techniques that execute the "Sharp With Receipts" voice. Pair with
`writing-principles` (the why) and the Voice spec in `weekly-blog/SKILL.md` (the what).

## Rhythm
- Vary sentence length deliberately. A long, building sentence that lays out the case, then a
  short one that drives it home.
- When every sentence follows the same pattern, the prose flattens and starts to read as
  generated. Break the pattern on purpose.
- Fragments are allowed when they land. Use them for emphasis, not as a tic.

## Concision
- Cut every word that does not sharpen the call. Adverbs that prop up weak verbs go first.
- One vivid, specific detail beats an exhaustive catalog. "Splitting third-down work with
  Kenneth Gainwell" outperforms "in a somewhat crowded backfield situation."
- Prefer the strong verb to the verb-plus-qualifier ("collapses" over "tends to go down").

## Concrete over abstract
- Name the player, the ADP, the round, the team. Always reach for the specific instance over
  the category. The receipt and the vividness are the same move.
- Filter the detail through the *argument's* attention: include what advances the call, leave
  out what merely decorates.

## Show vs. tell
- Show for the claims that carry the post; tell for logistics and transitions (a one-line
  setup is fine, do not dramatize it).
- Replace emotional labels with the observable fact. Not "this is a concerning trend," but the
  number that makes it concerning.

## Openings and closings
- Open on the tension or the claim itself. Never a definition, never "In best ball, …".
- State the thesis by the second paragraph.
- Land the close on a line that resolves the argument. Do not summarize what you just said.

## Punctuation discipline
- Default to commas, colons, semicolons, and full stops. Reserve the em dash for a genuine
  interruption (soft cap ~1 per 150 words, per ADR-001). If you reach for an em dash, first
  try restructuring the sentence around its real clause relationship.

## Roughness over polish
The trained instinct is to over-write: to buff every sentence to column-shine and reach for the
quotable line. That polish is itself an AI tell. Write rougher and narrower than feels finished.
- **Under-write, don't over-write.** Say the thing once, at the smallest scope that's true. A
  narrower claim ("I think people misread this") reads more human than the omniscient sweep
  ("most drafters read it backwards").
- **One image per sentence.** Never pair two idioms in one breath ("paid full freight … for
  scraps"). Pick the better one; cut the other.
- **State the claim plainly.** Skip the "it's not X, it's Y" pivot and the escalating triple
  ("real, structural, and not going to correct itself"). Make the point in one clause and move.
- **Distrust the performative line.** "Said the quiet part out loud," "nobody has to sweat on a
  Sunday," "the fish" — if a phrase sounds like a closing zinger, it probably reads as machine
  polish. Prefer the rough, domain-native way a sharp drafter would actually say it.
