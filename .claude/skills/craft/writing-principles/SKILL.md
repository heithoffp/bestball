# Writing Principles (blog craft reference)

> Adapted 2026-05-28 from `haowjy/creative-writing-skills` (`skills/writing-principles`),
> reframed from fiction to opinionated best-ball strategy writing. This is a reference
> module read by `weekly-blog` and the `blog-*` agents — not a user-invocable skill.

The governing fact: a best-ball post is an **argument**, not a story. It makes a falsifiable
draft call and asks the reader to act on it or argue back. Every principle below serves that.

## 1. Trust the reader
The reader reconstructs the argument as they go. Resist the trained impulse to explain every
step, resolve every caveat, and name every implication. That impulse is the single biggest
source of AI-sounding prose. State the fact and let it land. A drafter does not need to be
told what a Round 4 pick is.

## 2. Economy
Every sentence either sharpens the call or gets cut. The best details do two jobs at once:
ground the claim **and** reveal the stance. Single-purpose connective tissue ("It is also
worth considering that...") is the prose that makes writing feel flat. Delete it.

## 3. The four reward channels (reframed for argument)
Protect all four at once:
- **Clarity** — the thesis and the evidence chain are easy to follow. (The argument analogue
  of narrative transportation.)
- **Aesthetic** — sentence-level pleasure: varied rhythm, exact word choice, a line that lands.
- **Credibility** — the writer reads as a sharp, specific, trustworthy mind. Conviction backed
  by receipts, never naked assertion. (The analogue of character/social simulation: the
  *author* is the character the reader is sizing up.)
- **Momentum** — pacing pulls the reader toward the verdict. No paragraph stalls or recaps.

## 4. Conviction is collateralized
The house voice is high-conviction and non-consensus. The guardrail that keeps that from
becoming "loud and wrong": **every strong claim is immediately backed by a concrete,
KB-sourced detail** — a player, an ADP, a round, a team, an expert panel's take. No assertion
floats free.

## 5. The counter-discipline (avoiding AI prose)
LLM training pulls toward *completeness*: covering every angle, naming every emotion,
resolving every ambiguity, balancing every hand. Good blog prose resists this:
- **Take the side.** Beat the weak counters. Leave exactly one honest counter standing (see
  §6) — do not both-sides your way out of a verdict, but do not knife every objection either.
- **Don't name the feeling.** "This is fascinating / intriguing / notable" tells the reader
  what to feel. Show why through the fact itself.
- **Reserve the em dash.** Readers now read em-dash overuse as an AI signature. Default to
  commas, colons, semicolons, and full stops; rewrite the sentence around its real clause
  relationships rather than substituting punctuation. Reserve the em dash for a genuine
  interruption or a sharp appositive. Soft cap: ~1 per 150 words.
- **Break symmetry.** Not every point arrives in a tidy group of three. Vary the shape.

### Constructions that read as machine-written
These are the cadences a trained model reaches for by default. Each is a tell on its own; in
combination they are a signature. Catch them in revision:
- **Stacked metaphors** — two images in one breath ("paid full freight … for scraps a few
  rounds on"; "a cannon arm bolted onto a goal-line battering ram"). One image at a time; cut
  the second.
- **"It's not X, it's Y" pivots** — "That price isn't the cost of certainty. It's a tax." An
  archetypal AI cadence. State the claim plainly instead.
- **Escalating triples** — "it's real, it's structural, and it isn't going to correct itself."
  Break the rhythm; one restrained clause.
- **Faux-inevitable conclusions** — "The rule writes itself." Own it as opinion ("to me the
  takeaway is simple").
- **Omniscient "most people" framing** — introduce a mechanism, imply a hidden insight, then
  broaden to "most drafters read it backwards." Speak first-person and narrower ("I think
  people misread this").
- **Single-framing-device overcommitment** — a controlling metaphor is fine, but don't
  thematically pile it on (tax / retail / booked / markdown / full freight). Let some
  sentences be plain.
- **Over-polished column cadence** — "nobody has to sweat on a Sunday," "said the quiet part
  out loud," "the fish." Performative polish is itself a tell. Prefer rough, domain-native
  phrasing.

## 6. Genuine uncertainty (mandatory)
A human analyst wanders into caveats, contradictions, and exceptions. AI argumentation runs
thesis → support → intensify → conclude with no friction. So: **every post must contain at
least one genuine caveat or counter that is *not* immediately knifed** — a real place the call
could be wrong, left standing. A steelman demolished in the same breath does **not** count.

This does not soften the voice. The post is still high-conviction; it still takes the side and
beats the weak objections (§5). The rule adds exactly *one honest beat*, not both-sidesing and
not a retreat into hedging. The line: you concede the call could be wrong **here, for this
reason**, and you let that sit — then you still make the call. One standing counter, not a
column of them.

## Self-check
Could a reader who disagrees with the call understand exactly where you stand and why, from
the post alone? Is every strong claim carrying a receipt? Did the verdict actually land, or
did the post trail off into summary? Is there exactly one honest counter the post lets stand
without demolishing it? Did any sentence stack two images, pivot on "it's not X, it's Y,"
run an escalating triple, or claim to know what "most people" think?
