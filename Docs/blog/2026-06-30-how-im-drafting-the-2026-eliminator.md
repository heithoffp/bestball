A lot of people about to draft the Underdog Eliminator are going to draft it like Best Ball Mania, and the format will quietly punish every one of those instincts. So instead of handing you a list of rules, I want to walk through a real one, pick by pick: a 12-team table I drafted from the 8 seat, live on [ChadCandles' stream](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QjIwAEb7-YQ&t=2460s), in a friends-and-family room of sharp, heavily-invested drafters. Eighteen picks, and every one of them bends to two facts about the format.

First, Week 1 is a 6-of-12 gate. You draft a 12-person pod and six advance. It behaves like a DFS double-up, where you only have to beat the median, except you can't recover from missing it: a 20th-percentile Week 1 score knocks you out roughly 99% of the time. Second, the money sits at the back. Reach Week 14 and you lock about $10K no matter what you score that week, then it climbs hard from there: $20K, $50K, and a final three that pays $76K, $100K, and $200K up top.

![Payouts are stacked at the back: reaching Week 14 is most of the game.](/blog/images/eliminator-payout-ladder-2026-06-23.svg)

Put those together and the board re-prices itself. In BBM I chase ceiling. In the Eliminator I protect a floor and back-weight my byes. The whole game is never owning a hole that hands me an automatic loss in a single week. Here's the team I ended with. See the shape before we start, because it's the target, not an accident.

Three QB, five RB, seven WR, three TE. That 3-5-7-3 is the build that won the 2025 Eliminator, and I didn't force it; I arrived at it by following the rules below. The other thing to watch is the byes. By the last pick my roster is a nearly clean rainbow from Week 5 to Week 14, my best players deliberately clustered on the two latest byes. Watch it fill in.

### Pick 1 · overall 8 — CeeDee Lamb (WR, DAL · bye 14)

`ADP 9.5 · still on the board: Amon-Ra St. Brown, Ashton Jeanty, Justin Jefferson`

![strip:Bye board after pick 1](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-01.svg)

Lamb owns the single best bye in the format. Week 14 is the only free zero on the calendar, because it lands after the $10K lock, so it's a hole I might never have to absorb before the money arrives. The later the bye, the higher the EV, and his is as late as it gets.

In a vacuum, and in BBM, I have Amon-Ra St. Brown a hair above Lamb. In the Eliminator I take Lamb over him every time, and I mean that literally here: Amon-Ra came off the board the very next pick, at 9. The pick that follows is exactly why I was happy to let him go. First square lit on my bye board, and it's the best one in the format.

### Pick 2 · overall 17 — De'Von Achane (RB, MIA · bye 6)

`ADP 15.6 · still on the board: Chase Brown, Jeremiyah Love, Drake London`

![strip:Bye board after pick 2](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-02.svg)

This is the answer to "why Lamb over Amon-Ra." My ideal start runs at least one back through the first three rounds, because RB volume is the less volatile production on the board; you know where the ball is going week-to-week. But my two best back options at this pick, Achane and Chase Brown, both sit on the Week 6 bye. So does Amon-Ra. So does Justin Jefferson. So does nearly half the early board.

![Where the first three rounds' byes land: six of the top 36 by ADP sit on the Week 6 minefield, and a league-high seven on Week 11's byemageddon — with a thin, safer middle in between.](/blog/images/eliminator-week6-minefield-2026-06-23.svg)

That pileup is both a curse and a blessing, and most of the skill in an Eliminator draft is telling which one you're looking at.

The curse is that it strangles your options. Those elite names aren't a menu I can order from freely; they're mutually exclusive in disguise. Take St. Brown at 1.08 and I can't also have Achane or Chase Brown at the turn. Pairing them would bury two of my best players under one Week 6 zero. One first-round bye decision quietly opens or closes a quarter of the board behind it.

The blessing is that the hole is shared, and after Week 1 this turns into a head-to-head game. If a big slice of the elite pool is off in Week 6, and it is, then the team across from me that week is probably a star or two light itself. A bye only really sinks you when it leaves you uniquely thin while the field plays full strength; a crowded bye is the opposite. A sparse one is the trap. Only Kansas City and Carolina sit out Week 5, so a star on that bye, like Rashee Rice, Kenneth Walker, or Tetairoa McMillan, likely leaves you the only manager in your matchup down an expensive piece while the guy across from you fields a full lineup. A lonely bye like that quietly costs more than the name on it. One or two early-bye players rarely sink you. Concentration does.

So I don't fade Week 6, I budget it. I'll own one Week 6 player here, and I can add a second later as long as it's a different position, so no single lineup spot ever goes dark. What I won't do is stack three or four of my best scorers onto one off week and hand a coin-flip opponent a free win in a week I still have to win. That's the whole reason Lamb (Week 14) goes at 1.08 and Achane (Week 6) here, and not the reverse: I spend my early-bye budget on Achane now, and I stay off Week 6 until the very last stretch, where a second one, a deep-room receiver rather than a back, comes cheap.

### Pick 3 · overall 32 — Josh Jacobs (RB, GB · bye 11)

`ADP 39.3 · still there: Chris Olave, Rashee Rice, Josh Allen`

![strip:Bye board after pick 3](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-03.svg)

Jacobs was the last back before a real tier cliff, and Eliminator rooms draft running backs up because everyone knows volume is the floor. An offseason legal controversy knocked his ADP into the high 30s, and that discount is my first reminder of what drafting this early really costs: I'm being asked to price a situation that hasn't played out, and which way it breaks won't be clear for weeks. I take the risk anyway, because he's the back I want and the tier is about to break, so I'd rather lock him at 32 than bet he slides back to me. Hold onto that discomfort, though. It's the whole story of my last pick.

The harder call was Jacobs or Rashee Rice, still on the board and the more gifted player on paper. What tipped it away from Rice is the bye I flagged two picks ago. He sits on the lonely Week 5, the sparse kind that leaves you a piece short while the manager across from you is whole, and that docks him for me no matter the talent. Jacobs lands on the crowded Week 11 instead, and it never collides with Achane's Week 6, so no week of mine goes dark. I also want the back more than the receiver here. Both point the same way, so the tiebreak goes to Jacobs. Two backs through three rounds, exactly where I want to be.

### Pick 4 · overall 41 — Garrett Wilson (WR, NYJ · bye 13)

`ADP 39.2 · still there: Malik Nabers, Cam Skattebo, David Montgomery`

![strip:Bye board after pick 4](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-04.svg)

Two receivers and two backs through four picks is the balance I want, and Wilson rounds it out cleanly. The Week 13 bye is as much of the draw as the talent. It's a second late one to set beside Lamb's 14, and this is where I start back-weighting in earnest, steering my mid-round picks toward Week 13 and 14 teams so the lineup keeps breathing through the most expensive survival weeks. I don't feel pressed to add a third back yet, either. The RB tier here is long and flat, a run from David Montgomery down through Bucky Irving and Treveyon Henderson, and a tier that deep means I can take the receiver I rate now and still expect to land one of those backs at the next turn or two.

It helps that the two names I'm passing on, Malik Nabers and Cam Skattebo, are both coming off injury, a profile I fade hard in a one-and-done format. When a single bad Week 1 ends the tournament, I don't want a starter whose floor still hinges on how a body holds up in September. Nabers especially is one I'm off almost entirely this year. Wilson raises none of those questions, so he's the easy pick.

### Pick 5 · overall 56 — Colston Loveland (TE, CHI · bye 10)

`ADP 46.0 · still there: Tony Pollard, Christian Watson, Jameson Williams`

![strip:Bye board after pick 5](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-05.svg)

The back I was hoping would slide back to me here never did. But the board didn't leave me empty-handed. This is the noisy June ADP working in my favor: a pure value pick. With no hole I'm forced to fill, I'm free to take whoever the board has mispriced worst, and that's Loveland. Rooms correctly bump backs up, which shoves a tight end like him below where he belongs, and he slid ten spots into my lap at 56. An elite tight end is one of the few players who can win you a week single-handedly, the rare position-busting score the guy across from you can't match at the spot, and that ceiling is a real plus this early. I don't force elite TE every draft. I take it when the market hands it to me.

The flip side is sitting right there in Jameson Williams, who keeps sliding, and for two reasons worth understanding. One, his Week 6 bye drops him onto the same minefield half the early board already sits on, so he's hard to slot cleanly. Two, his profile is the opposite of what this format rewards: big spikes stacked on a floor that falls out from under him, with seven games under 7 full-PPR points in 2025. In a survival contest, where one quiet week ends your run, that boom-bust shape is exactly the profile to be wary of.

### Pick 6 · overall 65 — Jaylen Warren (RB, PIT · bye 9)

`ADP 75.2 · still there: Joe Burrow, Drake Maye, Carnell Tate, Jalen Hurts`

![strip:Bye board after pick 6](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-06.svg)

This is typically where I like to take my first quarterback, but I'm letting the run pass. Look at what went in the seven picks after me: Burrow, Drake Maye, and Hurts, three startable QBs, all gone. That's the format in one frame. QB scoring is compressed and replacement level is high, so I'm fine waiting, and this draft I have a specific reason to wait exactly one more pick. I'm hoping to stack Dak Prescott with Lamb next round, so my quarterback is already spoken for. I passed on Carnell Tate too, the top rookie receiver left on the board. Rookie production tends to be back-weighted, ramping as the year goes on, and that's backwards for a contest that can end in Week 1; if he starts slow, the upside shows up months after I've already been knocked out.

What I take instead is the projectable Week 1 back volume I can't afford to fall behind on, even reaching ten picks to get it. Three backs now, on byes 6, 9, and 11, no two alike.

### Pick 7 · overall 80 — Dak Prescott (QB, DAL · bye 14)

`ADP 79.7 · still there: J.K. Dobbins, Jayden Reed`

![strip:Bye board after pick 7](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-07.svg)

Two ideas land on this pick. The first is the free stack. Dak plus Lamb is a skinny stack, a quarterback and one pass-catcher, which is all the correlation this format wants. Because both are Cowboys on the Week 14 bye, doubling that bye costs nothing; they only zero out after the lock. This Lamb-and-Dak pairing is close to the signature build of the format.

The second is why now, and why skinny. With Caleb Williams just off the board, the QB tier was thinning, so this was the window. But I still take only the arm and one target. Overstacking is a season-long move that makes spike weeks compound; in a single head-to-head week, three teammates cannibalize the same touches and drag my average down. Too many eggs in one basket is far more dangerous here than in BBM. QB-14 lights up. With Dak in, four of my players now sit on the Week 13 and 14 byes, and the back of my schedule is covered.

### Pick 8 · overall 89 — Chris Godwin (WR, TB · bye 10)

`ADP 89.3 · still there: Quentin Johnston, Kyle Monangai, Jaxson Dart`

![strip:Bye board after pick 8](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-08.svg)

Godwin rounds out my receiver room, and what I want from a WR3 here is floor, not ceiling. Quentin Johnston was sitting right there, and on talent it's close, but his profile is the same boom-or-bust shape I flagged with Jameson Williams: big games and not much underneath them. In a format where one quiet week ends me, I'd rather have a receiver I can count on for catches, and Godwin's target floor is exactly that. With Mike Evans gone, he's the established target in a Tampa room otherwise full of year-one and year-two players who may take time to produce, so the volume should funnel to him early, when this contest is actually decided.

### Pick 9 · overall 104 — Patrick Mahomes (QB, KC · bye 5)

`ADP 89.6 · still there: Bo Nix, Tucker Kraft, Wan'Dale Robinson`

![strip:Bye board after pick 9](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-09.svg)

Honestly, I was hoping Rachaad White would get back to me here. He's the last back in his tier, and he went one pick before me, at 103. So I pivoted to the best value on the board, and it wasn't close: Mahomes carries a 90.6 ADP in Best Ball Mania, and Eliminator rooms had pushed him all the way to 104. A fourteen-pick discount on an great quarterback is not a price I talk myself out of.

His Week 5 bye and the injury question are the reasons he was sitting there, and at this number neither scares me off. I fade injury as a rule, but Mahomes is projected to start Week 1, so the one risk that can actually eliminate me is already off the table. Week 5 is also the earliest bye on my roster, and he's the only player on it, so he papers the front of the calendar that Dak's Week 14 can't reach instead of doubling a hole. I'd pair him with a Chiefs receiver if the value lined up, but correlation is a minor edge in this format, not something I'll reach for, so the empty stack doesn't bother me. Two arms now, byes 5 and 14, about as far apart as the schedule allows.

### Picks 10 & 11 · Ricky Pearsall (WR, SF · bye 8) and Tyrone Tracy (RB, NYG · bye 8)

`Pearsall — ADP 102.4, fell to 113` · `Tracy — ADP 131.7, taken at 128`

![strip:Bye board after pick 11](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-11.svg)

The middle rounds have exactly one bar in this format: a plausible Week 1 role. Pearsall is an ascending target share in a real offense, not a November-breakout dart. Tracy has a defined early-down role and locked Week 1 touches, not a handcuff lottery ticket. Both clear the bar.

Pearsall opens Week 8 on my receiver board; Tracy shares that Week 8, but he's a back, so within his own room he's clean (my backs now sit on 6, 8, 9, and 11). The no-shared-bye rule applies inside a position, not across the whole roster. And notice the one bye week my roster still hasn't touched anywhere: Week 7. Hold that thought; it matters at the very end.

### Pick 12 · overall 137 — KC Concepcion (WR, CLE · bye 11)

`ADP 116.8 · still there: Jalen McMillan, Tre Tucker`

![strip:Bye board after pick 12](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-12.svg)

Now the rookie fade, and its one exception. Late-developing rookies are wrong for Eliminator because their value lands in November, after most entries are already gone. Closing a draft with a stack of them is the fanciest way to take four zeros. The exception is a rookie with a locked Week 1 role, and that's the only reason Concepcion is here: I need him to profile as a Week 1 contributor in Cleveland, not as an October thesis. Price is the other half of it. Carnell Tate would have charged me a sixth-round pick to take on rookie uncertainty, too steep for me; Concepcion asks a twelfth-rounder for the same kind of risk, and six rounds cheaper I'll wear it far more readily. If his snap share read murky to me, I'd pass. It's a calculated bet, and I'll call it a bet. His Week 11 bye lands on byemageddon, the overlap I said I'd gladly take.

### Pick 13 · overall 152 — Tyler Allgeier (RB, ARI · bye 14)

`ADP 157.8 · still there: Tyjae Spears, Geno Smith`

![strip:Bye board after pick 13](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-13.svg)

Lamb and Dak already give me the Week 14 bye twice, so why reach for a third? Because a Week 14 back costs nothing to stash: his only blank is the free week after the lock, which means he's guaranteed to be active in every week that can meaningfully eliminate me. Tyjae Spears was the better standalone back sitting there. Allgeier was the better Eliminator pick.

### Picks 14 & 15 · Hunter Henry (TE, NE · bye 11) and Gunnar Helm (TE, TEN · bye 9)

`Henry — ADP 150.1` · `Helm — ADP 178.1`

![strip:Bye board after pick 15](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-15.svg)

The third tight end and third quarterback exist for one reason: to keep me off a zero at a position I only start one of. A blank at a onesie in a one-on-one week is basically a loss, so I'm not chasing upside, I'm buying a body with a real target share and a bye that fits. Henry's Week 11 sits clean against Loveland's Week 10. Then Helm completes the set: with my three tight ends on byes 9, 10, and 11, a tight end bye never leaves me thin. Even with running back darts like Alvin Kamara on the board, I took the tight end whose bye actually fit. This is the puzzle being solved on purpose, not hoped into.

### Pick 16 · overall 185 — Malik Washington (WR, MIA · bye 6)

`ADP 192.3 · Eliminator move -3 · still there: Jaydon Blue, Germie Bernard`

![strip:Bye board after pick 16](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-16.svg)

Even at receiver depth I want a defined role over a lottery ticket, and Washington has a real slot path in Miami. His Week 6 bye makes him a second Week-6 player, but he's a receiver and Achane is a back, so neither room doubles up. Two of my players off in Week 6, split across two positions. Fine.

### Pick 17 · overall 200 — Deshaun Watson (QB, CLE · bye 11)

`ADP 213.4 · Eliminator move -8 · value at 200 · the only arms left after this: Michael Penix, Kirk Cousins`

![strip:Bye board after pick 17](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-17.svg)

With Mahomes on 5 and Dak on 14, my third quarterback only has to cover the middle of the calendar, and Watson's Week 11 bye does exactly that. Three QB byes on 5, 11, and 14, fully staggered. This is also where I lean into an edge instead of fighting it: late quarterback rooms are messy, Cleveland's especially, and the third QB slot is the one place that ambiguity is a feature rather than a bug. It's insurance, so I take the arm whose bye fills my last open week and don't sweat the depth-chart noise the way I would for a starter. He's a better pick here than in Best Ball Mania, too, where a quarterback who could lose his job by midseason is close to dead weight. In the Eliminator I only ask two things of a QB3, and Watson covers both: he's Cleveland's projected Week 1 starter, a live body when the gate slams, and even if the job slips away from him later, the week I most want the extra arm, Mahomes's Week 5 bye, comes long before any benching would. The board forced the timing too, with the QB shelf about to empty. 

### Pick 18 · overall 209 — Brandon Aiyuk (WR, FA · proj. bye 7)

`ADP 191.6 · Eliminator move +9 · fell to my last pick at 209 · an unsigned free agent who may not play in 2026`

![strip:Bye board after pick 18](/blog/images/eliminator-byeboard-18.svg)

This last pick is the whole case for drafting in August instead of late June, and it's the bookend to the worry I parked back at Jacobs. Prices right now are guesses about situations that haven't happened yet. Jacobs's legal cloud was the mild version of that, and Aiyuk is the same gap at its most extreme: an unsigned free agent who may not play a snap in the NFL this year. I'm taking him at 209 knowing exactly that. The earlier you draft, the more of these blanks you're forced to price, and the Eliminator punishes a blank harder than any format, because a player who never suits up is a standing zero at his position, and a zero in a single elimination week is a loss.

That's why a swing like this belongs only on the last pick, at my deepest position. If Aiyuk never signs, I'm out a seventh receiver I was never going to start most weeks anyway, a zero I can absorb. Make the same bet earlier, or at a onesie like quarterback or tight end, and that same uncertainty becomes the hole that ends your season. If he does land somewhere, Washington is the bet, and his Week 7 bye is the one week my roster doesn't otherwise touch, so he would round out the rainbow rather than dent it. Either way the real move is to re-pull in August, when the signings and depth charts are real and these June guesses turn back into prices.

![The finished bye board: a clean rainbow from Week 5 to Week 14 with no in-room collisions, the best players stacked on the two latest byes. The dashed square is Aiyuk, a free agent whose Week 7 bye is projected on a Washington landing.](/blog/images/eliminator-bye-rainbow-2026-06-23.svg)

That rainbow is the plan made visible, and you can watch your own fill in the same way: Eliminator Mode in the [Draft Assistant](/draft-assistant) lights up which weeks each room is off and where two players collide as you draft.

Step back and the finished board reads the way the table at the top promised: a clean rainbow from Week 5 to Week 14, no two players in a room sharing a week, my best players (Lamb, Garrett Wilson, Dak, Allgeier) on the two latest byes. The only loose thread is Aiyuk's Week 7, which holds if he signs where I expect. That is back-weight and stagger run across eighteen picks, and the 3-5-7-3 shape it produced is the build that won last year, reached by following the rules instead of forcing a template.

Here's where I could be wrong, and I'm not going to talk myself out of it. This whole build is a June bet on Week 1 roles that haven't settled. Concepcion has to actually win the Cleveland job. Pearsall has to be healthy and on the field, and Aiyuk has to sign somewhere with a real Week 1 role at all. Tracy's early-down work has to hold. If two or three of those break the wrong way by September, the floor I drafted for isn't really there, and a thin June board is exactly where that risk hides. 

The spine holds either way: survive Week 1, reach Week 14, never own a hole you can't afford. Get those three right and the staggered byes do the rest, keeping a full lineup alive into the weeks where the money actually sits.
