Pull up the DraftKings board next to Underdog and the two mostly agree: the same names in roughly the same places, the median top-100 player within two and a half picks of the same spot on both sites. But a handful of pass-catchers break the pattern and come steadily cheaper on DraftKings, and the easy read is that it's a trick of the scoring. The easy read costs you the five best values on the board.

DraftKings is full PPR; Underdog is half. A catch is worth twice as much on DraftKings, so its board should tilt toward the players who catch the ball, not away from them. That's the tell. On the site that pays double for a reception, certain receivers and receiving backs keep coming cheaper than they go on Underdog. Here are the five sales worth crossing platforms for. Every ADP in this piece comes from [my own tracker](/adp-tracker), both boards pulled the same morning, 2026-06-09, and the list runs smallest sale to biggest.

First, the control, because it's how you know the scoring isn't the cause. Derrick Henry caught [15 passes all of last season](https://www.nfl.com/players/derrick-henry/stats/) and slides from 17.6 on Underdog to 20.4 on DraftKings, 16% off his price. That's correct: a back who only runs is worth less on a site that pays for catches. Now the other direction. Christian McCaffrey caught [102 balls last season](https://www.nfl.com/players/christian-mccaffrey/stats/), and DraftKings prices him up for it, 6.3 against his 7.3 on Underdog. Full PPR moved both backs the way it should, the receiving back up and the pure runner down. So when a pass-catcher comes cheaper on DK anyway, the scoring already did its job, and the gap that's left is the market missing.

![The control says the scoring works: Henry's slide is earned and McCaffrey gets priced up for his 102 catches. The five gold bars are pass-catchers sliding anyway.](/blog/images/dk-discounts-2026-06-09.svg)

### 5. Bucky Irving, down 11%

`Underdog 53.2 → DraftKings 59.2 · 6.0 picks later`

[Thirty catches in just 10 games](https://www.nfl.com/players/bucky-irving/stats/) before his year ended early, a 50-catch pace, real passing-down work on a site that pays for it. Here's the honest hole, and I'm not filling it: that receiving role is the exact thing Kenneth Gainwell is built to take, and the Tampa staff has talked up a committee all spring. The discount pays you for part of that risk; you carry the rest, which is why he's fifth and not higher.

### 4. Tetairoa McMillan, down 11%

`Underdog 37.9 → DraftKings 42.3 · 4.4 picks later`

[Seventy catches and a thousand yards across all 17 games as a rookie](https://www.nfl.com/players/tetairoa-mcmillan/stats/). A floor like that in year two is the profile the format pays most, and DraftKings sells it four and a half picks cheaper than Underdog does.

### 3. Jordan Addison, down 12%

`Underdog 86.3 → DraftKings 96.3 · 10.0 picks later`

Ten full picks on a player whose situation just got better. Kyler Murray is throwing him the ball now instead of J.J. McCarthy, and Minnesota added no real target competition in the draft; with Jalen Nailor gone, the target room got thinner. Addison caught [42 balls in 14 games](https://www.nfl.com/players/jordan-addison/stats/) last year, a 51-catch pace over a full season. Underdog calls him an eighth-rounder. On DraftKings the same player, in a better offense, is still there at the round-nine turn.

### 2. Mike Evans, down 17%

`Underdog 44.6 → DraftKings 52.0 · 7.4 picks later`

The biggest receiver sale, and the one with a real story under it: Evans is a 49er now, and a receiver who is both aging and on a new team is easy to fade twice. I'd take the other side. He caught [30 balls in 8 games last season](https://www.nfl.com/players/mike-evans/stats/), a 64-catch pace. A 17% discount on the site that values receivers most reads to me like the age and the move priced in twice.

### 1. TreVeyon Henderson, down 19%

`Underdog 48.6 → DraftKings 57.9 · 9.3 picks later`

The widest sale on the board. [Thirty-five catches across all 17 games as a rookie](https://www.nfl.com/players/treveyon-henderson/stats/), and he goes nine picks and 19% later on DraftKings than on Underdog. He's a mid-round name in the heart of the board, where every pick is contested and prices mean something. A back who catches that much should cost more on the catch-paying site, not 19% less. That's the clearest miss of the five.

![Every pair runs the same direction: later on the site that pays double for the catch.](/blog/images/dk-sales-board-2026-06-09.svg)

---

Now the caveat, because a discount list needs one. These are one morning's prices, and a gap this size can narrow by the weekend. The deeper the pick, the noisier the comparison gets, too. Travis Hunter goes 16 picks later on DraftKings and I make nothing of it, and Addison at pick 96 sits closest to that noise, which is why his case leans on the offense around him. What the noise can't explain is the top of this list: a 19% fade on a 35-catch back in the middle of the fifth round needs a reason, and the scoring isn't one.

DraftKings pays double for a catch and then keeps discounting the players who make them. I draft it the way the math says the room won't: these five, every time the board hands them back to me.
