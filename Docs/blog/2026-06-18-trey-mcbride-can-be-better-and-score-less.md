At the second-to-third-round turn right now, Trey McBride is one of the best players on the board and close to the worst pick in his range.

He goes 26.3 on Underdog and a touch earlier, 23.6, on DraftKings, a top-30 cost on both sites. He earns the talent. What the price assumes is that Arizona feeds him last year's volume again, and I don't think it will. McBride can be a better football player in 2026 and still score fewer fantasy points, because the number that built his 2025 was manufactured by the offense around him, not by the player. Every ADP here comes from [my own tracker](/adp-tracker), both boards pulled the same morning, 2026-06-15; numbers are Underdog unless I say otherwise.

Start with what you're paying for. McBride caught [126 balls for 1,239 yards and 11 touchdowns across all 17 games last season](https://www.nfl.com/players/trey-mcbride/stats/), a receiving line most teams would take from a WR1. It happened on an offense that threw to two people. Arizona dropped back 40 to 50 times a game and funneled nearly all of it to McBride and Michael Wilson, and late in games, with the score settled, the Cardinals kept feeding him to chase the record book. His 31.4 half-PPR explosion in Week 15 came out of exactly that. The shape of the 126 catches gives it away: they came at [just 9.8 yards a grab](https://www.nfl.com/players/trey-mcbride/stats/), the short, high-volume checkdown work of an offense with no third option to throw to. The catches were real. The reason for them was a target tree that won't grow back the same way.

That's the distinction the 26 price misses. The bet isn't on McBride the player; it's on the offense staying a two-man passing game. Add a third option, get healthier at receiver, settle the quarterback room, and the target share that produced 126 catches normalizes even if McBride is sharper than he was. A realistic down year for him lands around 13.5 PPR points a game. That's a perfectly good tight end and a bad return on a pick this early.

If I'm paying elite-tight-end freight, I want it leaning on a real offense, not on one team's willingness to force a stat line. Brock Bowers is that bet. His case is the quarterback and the line in front of him: Geno Smith with real upside behind him, and better protection than Arizona gives McBride. Bowers is the one who had the down, banged-up year, [64 catches for 680 yards and 7 touchdowns in 12 games](https://www.nfl.com/players/brock-bowers/stats/), and the market is still paying up for him: 19.8 on Underdog, a full six picks ahead of McBride. The board already treats the structurally safer player as the TE1. I'd take that lean and let McBride go.

Here's the part I can't solve, and I'm not going to pretend I can. McBride's price doesn't move. He's sat at this turn all spring, and "wait for the discount" only works if the discount ever shows up. There are two tight ends worth building a roster around, and if the room hands me McBride at 26 and Bowers is already gone, fading him means leaving the position to a ten-round wait and a Colston Loveland dart. I still have McBride a hair ahead of Bowers as a player, too. The talent was never the question. On the boards where he's the last elite anchor standing, I'll probably hold my nose and take him, and I'd tell you to do the same.

So this isn't a fade at any cost. I just won't pay 2025's box score for 2026's offense. McBride will be very good again. He may even be better. He just isn't going to catch 126 balls on a team that won't be scamming the back half of games to get them to him, and 26 is the price of believing he will.
