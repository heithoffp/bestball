Everybody wants Josh Allen, and it's easy to see why. He ran for 14 touchdowns last year and 12 the year before (nfl.com, accessed 2026-05-29), which is elite passing volume plus a goal-line rushing package, and you almost never get the "what happened to my quarterback this week" game out of him. He's going 24th overall, the tail end of the second round, and he's the only quarterback anyone takes that early (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). He sits alone, two rounds clear of the next name.

People are paying for the comfort of not thinking about quarterback. That's the Allen tax, and this year I'd skip it.

The elite quarterback tier in 2026 is the deepest it's been in years, and most of it is cheap. Lamar Jackson goes at 46. Joe Burrow at 53. Jalen Hurts is at 68 overall, round six (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). For a quarterback with his rushing ceiling, that price is wild.

Fifteen rushing touchdowns is the whole reason you'd pay up at the position. Hurts has done exactly that, running for 15 in 2023 and 14 the season after (nfl.com, accessed 2026-05-29), the same number that makes Allen feel safe. You can get very similar upside several rounds later this year.

Here's the part I won't talk you out of. Last season Hurts ran for 8 while Allen ran for 14 (nfl.com, accessed 2026-05-29). The ceiling I'm selling you on Hurts is two years old; Allen's floor is from this past season. If what wins your weeks is a rushing quarterback who finds the end zone no matter what the matchup looks like, there's a real edge in Allen, and I might be underrating it. Paying for that floor isn't a crazy thing to do.

I still land on skipping him, and the reason is what the 24th pick costs you everywhere else. Spend it on a quarterback and you've passed on a top-15 running back or a high-volume receiver, in a year where running back is genuinely thin. Wait on the position and you get four extra rounds building the spots that decide leagues while the drafter next to you already sank his early capital into QB. The elite-QB tier is cheap enough now that you can pair one of these names with any running-back plan you want.

Why does the discount exist at all? Stacking demand, mostly, not the quarterback himself. A lot of QB pricing is set by how many drafters are fighting over his pass-catchers. Dak Prescott holds his price because CeeDee Lamb and George Pickens pull two different drafters toward him. Caleb Williams has Luther Burden, Colston Loveland, and Rome Odunze. Hurts has Devonta Smith and basically nobody else, so his price slides further than a crowded-stack QB's. The names I want most come cheap because fewer drafters are fighting over their receivers, not because the quarterback is worse.

The other thing holding Hurts down is offseason noise: reports that he wanted designed runs cut from the game plan, and that Philadelphia might take him off the Tush Push. It reads as exactly that, noise. A team that wants to win and a quarterback who wants to keep his job both point the same direction. I don't think this gap closes by August.

It already plays out in live drafts. One drafter paid up for Allen as his QB1 early, stacked him with two of his receivers, and then watched Lamar Jackson fall into his lap as a second quarterback later in the same draft.

To me the takeaway is pretty simple. Don't spend a top-five-round pick at quarterback in 2026 unless you're specifically building a Lamar Jackson stack. Everywhere else, let the position come to you around round six, where Hurts, Mahomes, and Dak are all sitting.

Allen will score his points; he always does. But I'm happy to let someone else spend a second-round pick on him. I'd rather have the four rounds.
