Luther Burden is going at pick 49 in best-ball drafts, a fourth-round receiver, and the case for him fits on a bumper sticker: yards-per-route-run king (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). The rate is real, and a fourth-round price says the market believes it.

I don't. Not because Burden is bad, but because of how that number got made. YPRR is only as trustworthy as the route count under it, and a rate built on thin usage hasn't settled. I read the sample before I read the rate, and on Burden the sample isn't there yet.

Start with the rule. A high YPRR off a handful of routes is a fade, not a buy. Burden's rate is a king's rate, but he never proved it across a full route tree, and you're paying fourth-round money for a figure that hasn't stabilized. There are too many receivers in his range carrying the same upside with a real season behind it. His reputation is priced higher than his sample has earned.

Now flip it. Davante Adams ran north of 1.90 YPRR last year across a full slate, and he's going at 50.6 (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). I trust that number without flinching, because the volume behind it leaves no room to call it luck. Stefon Diggs is the same story, twelfth in the NFL in YPRR on a full season of routes, and he's sitting at 145.2 while he waits to sign somewhere (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). The efficiency is real and the price is nearly free. A full season of routes turns a rate into evidence; a handful of them leaves you a guess.

The hardest version of the rule is the ugly number that won't wash off. Rome Odunze ran 624 routes as a rookie and posted a 1.18 YPRR. That is a bad figure, and the volume is what makes it frightening. You cannot file 624 routes under small-sample noise and move on. It comes in worse than Xavier Worthy's rookie rate, and it feeds a quieter, nastier read: maybe he just was never good. The market has him at 60.8, a tick ahead of Adams' efficiency tier, betting the rate was circumstance (FantasyPros best-ball, accessed 2026-05-29). That bet runs against the sample. A poor rate earned on heavy volume is the most trustworthy number on this whole list.

The rule has to cut the other way too, or it's just a stick for beating up young receivers. A low number on thin usage earns the same skepticism a flashy one does. The same logic even works on a running back's per-carry stats: Bhayshul Tuten's missing explosive runs come on a short carry count, the kind of sample that swings on where and how often a back runs. A weak number there isn't proof of a weakness yet. Low isn't automatically bad, high isn't automatically good. Volume is what turns a rate into a fact.

Here is where I could be wrong on Odunze, and I'm not going to talk you out of it. His rookie year was really two seasons. Through Week 8 he ran a 26% target share and averaged 16 PPR points a game, a top-ten receiver's pace, and then a foot injury arrived and the rest of the year was unusable. Those 624 routes include a long stretch of a hurt player dragging the rate down. The full-season figure might be burying a healthy receiver under an injured one, and the heavy-volume rule has no clean answer for that. If you trust the first half, Odunze at 60.8 is a value, and I understand the drafter who takes him there.

I still read the sample before the rate. The injury split is a reason to hesitate on Odunze, not a reason to throw the rule out, and the rule is what stops me from paying Burden's fourth-round price for a number nobody has confirmed. So fade the YPRR king until the routes back the reputation, trust Adams and Diggs because a full season already did, and stop treating Odunze's 1.18 as a typo.
